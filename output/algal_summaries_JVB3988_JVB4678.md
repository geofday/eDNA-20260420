**JVB3988** · Five sites · East Machias Watershed, ME · October 2024

Both batches now carry complete fish (MiFish 12S) + algal (23S rRNA) panels. JVB4678 spans a wide geographic and seasonal range — a Cape Cod coastal pond (September), Manchester-by-the-Sea Sawmill Brook (October and March), and the Orange River in Maine (March ice-out).

School Street vs. Elm Street on the same March day: a tidal-freshwater gradient captured in a single batch — the tidal reach dominated by estuarine marine algae, the freshwater reach by cold-water chrysophytes.

# BATCH JVB3988 — East Machias Watershed, ME
## October 2024 · Five Sites

The five JVB3988 sites sample the lower and middle reaches of the East Machias River and its major tributaries in Washington County, Maine — one of the most remote and least-developed coastal watersheds in the northeastern United States. Sampling in October coincides with two ecologically significant events: the beginning of the Atlantic Salmon spawning run into natal tributaries, and peak autumn leaf-fall from the surrounding spruce-fir-hardwood forest. Both signals appear in the algal data. *Streptophyta* reads at several sites reflect terrestrial plant DNA entering the stream in leaf litter and throughfall — a seasonal sampling-date effect, not impairment. *Chamaesiphon*, an epiphytic cyanobacterium found only in clean cold flowing water attached to algal filaments, appears at two sites — a signal of high physical habitat quality.

### Narrative

Northern Stream is the most ecologically significant algal sample in JVB3988. The combination of *Mougeotia* (6.9%), boreal spruce eDNA (3.4%), and *Chamaesiphon* (2.4%) constitutes a biological certificate of remote, cold, clean, headwater stream quality — the kind of water chemistry that once characterized all of coastal Maine and now survives intact primarily in Washington County.

*Mougeotia* is a filamentous conjugating green alga (Streptophyta) that grows in dense green mats on rocks and submerged wood in cold, soft, slightly acidic streams. It is the most common and most reliable soft-acid-cold-water macroalgal indicator in the Maine headwater literature. Its co-occurrence with *Chamaesiphon* — which requires stable, clean substrate and cold flowing water for attachment — makes the water quality interpretation unambiguous. *Picea* DNA at 3.4% is not an aquatic organism; it is spruce pollen and needle debris entering the stream from surrounding vegetation. Because spruce-fir forest is present at essentially every Maine sampling location in this study, *Picea* eDNA appearing at only one site reflects stochastic non-target capture, not a site-specific diagnostic — terrestrial plant eDNA in stream samples should not be used as a watershed-character indicator.

The Fallfish at 85.1% dominate the fish library in this clean-gravel headwater tributary. The supporting cyprinid assemblage — Creek Chub (1.6%), Blacknose Dace (1.4%), *Luxilus* spp. (2.0%) — corroborates the algal inference of clean, flowing, cold water. The Atlantic Salmon at 1.0% are the biological confirmation of everything the algae suggested: this is a natal tributary in good enough condition to attract returning adult Salmon on their spawning run.

### Narrative

*Batrachospermum* is the most informative single taxon in the JVB3988 algal dataset. This filamentous freshwater red alga forms distinctive gelatinous tufts on rocks and submerged wood in cold, clean, fast-flowing, oxygen-rich streams. It has essentially no tolerance for warm temperatures, low dissolved oxygen, or unstable substrate. It grows slowly and is easily displaced by algal blooms or sedimentation. Its detection in Beaver Dam Stream — even at trace levels (0.9%) — constitutes a direct biological measurement of water quality that no chemical indicator can match for ecological specificity.

*Chamaesiphon* at 12.0% reinforces this interpretation. *Chamaesiphon* is a unicellular cyanobacterium that lives epiphytically attached to filamentous algae in clean, cool, flowing water. Unlike bloom-forming cyanobacteria, it does not float, does not produce toxins, and is not associated with eutrophication. Its abundance in this sample — 12% of all algal reads — signals stable, well-oxygenated, flowing habitat.

*Ulnaria ulna* at 8.4% is a planktonic/tychoplankton diatom that indicates moderate current velocity and some nutrient availability. Its presence is consistent with the Creek Chub and Dace fish community — a headwater stream with sufficient flow to maintain aquatic insects, fish, and attached algae.

*Palmaria* sp. at 2.0% is a red alga primarily found in marine environments. Its detection in an inland stream is almost certainly a QC artifact (coastal marine aerosol, reagent contamination) or a database misassignment. *Halamphora coffeaeformis* at 13.8% is similarly suspect at an inland headwater site — more likely a closely related freshwater *Halamphora* than the marine taxon.

The Creek Chub (47.1%) and Dace (10.9%) are consistent with headwater stream character in the mid-reach — above tidal influence, below the coldest headwater springs. Both require clean water and stable substrate.
