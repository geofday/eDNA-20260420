# eDNA Field Reports — Batch JVB5403
## 23S rRNA (Algae) + MiFish 12S rRNA (Fish) — Integrated Ecological Narratives

**Batch JVB5403** · Lab results: October 10, 2025 · Four sites · East Machias watershed + Orange River, ME

All four sites carry both fish (MiFish 12S) and algal (23S rRNA) panels. Sites span two distinct systems: the East Machias watershed (Gardner Lake, Northern Inlet, East Machias Rt 9) and the Orange River headwater corridor (Orange River, June 2025).

**Brook Trout status across all four sites: Absent.** Algal communities at three of four sites carry strong cold-oligotrophic water quality signals that are entirely consistent with Brook Trout habitat requirements. The fish community, not the water chemistry, is the limiting factor at these locations — suggesting mainstem sampling position, seasonal masking, or the species being upstream of the sampling point.

### Narrative

Gardner Lake in early August presents a textbook mesotrophic-to-weakly-eutrophic summer lake signal. The 32% Cyanobacteria is driven almost entirely by picocyanobacteria — *Synechococcus* (multiple strains, ~7% combined) and *Cyanobium* (3.5%) — rather than the large-colony bloom-forming taxa (*Microcystis*, *Anabaena*) that generate toxic HABs. This distinction matters: picocyanobacteria are nutrient and light indicators, not immediate public health flags. However, the presence of *Chrysochromulina* (Haptophyta, 2.9%) at this density is worth tracking — this genus is potentially toxic at bloom levels and has caused fish kills in Scandinavian lakes. At 2.9% it is not alarming; rising in a future sample would be.

The dominant fish signal — Eel 54%, Yellow Perch 32% — is consistent with a warm-stratified lake in late summer. American Eel are catadromous and can be abundant in lake systems anywhere in the coastal drainage. Yellow Perch at 32% confirms a warm productive epilimnion. The Alewife at 7.9% is the lake-run form: Alewife that complete their entire life cycle in freshwater landlocked systems rather than migrating to sea. Rainbow Smelt at 0.3% — though a trace — is significant: Rainbow Smelt require cold, well-oxygenated water for overwintering and spawning, and their presence confirms the hypolimnion of Gardner Lake remains cold enough to support cold-water species year-round, even if the surface is warm in August.

**No Brook Trout detected.** Gardner Lake at 50-ft depth in the main basin is not unexpected territory for Brook Trout absence — this is a warm-water zone in summer. Tributaries feeding the lake's cold inlet arms would be the priority sampling locations for Brook Trout detection.

### Native vs. Non-Native

All fish detected are native to Maine. *Chrysochromulina* in the algal community is native. No introduced or stocked species detected.

### Narrative

Northern Inlet is the ecological standout in JVB5403 — not because of its fish community diversity but because of what the algae reveal about the water itself. The near-total absence of Cyanobacteria (0.2%) is diagnostic: this water is cold, nutrient-poor, and well-oxygenated. The dominant taxa are *Trachydiscus minutus* (a cold soft-water xanthophyte uncommon enough to be a meaningful indicator), *Mallomonas* (the gold-standard chrysophyte for oligotrophic cold acidic water), and *Chromulina* — a chrysophyte assemblage that, when this dominant, essentially constitutes a biological certificate of clean water.

*Halamphora coffeaeformis* at 8.8% is a database assignment artifact here, consistent with the pattern documented across all East Machias sites in JVB3988 and JVB5403: the site is many miles above tidal influence, and the barcode matches a freshwater *Halamphora* species to the marine taxon because the freshwater taxon is underrepresented in the reference database. The corrected fish community — *Lepomis* spp. dominant (23.5%) alongside Sucker, Eel, *Ameiurus*, and *Micropterus* — is a diverse warm-water native assemblage, consistent with a lower-reach outlet site with some lentic habitat patches. The high algal quality signal and the diverse native fish community together indicate an ecologically healthy but not cold-headwater reach.

The water chemistry signal here — exceptional oligotrophy confirmed by *Trachydiscus* and *Mallomonas* — indicates that the watershed feeding this site is cold, forested, and largely undisturbed. Brook Trout habitat conditions are likely present in the headwater tributaries above this reach; sampling at higher elevation in the Northern Inlet drainage basin would be the next logical step.

### Native vs. Non-Native

All species native. *Halamphora coffeaeformis* is a marine/brackish native diatom. No non-native species detected.

The Orange River in June low-flow carries the cleanest algal water quality signal in the JVB5403 batch — and the clearest cold-oligotrophic signal the Orange River has produced across any of its six time-point samples. ### Narrative

The Orange River in June 2025 — under low-flow, exposed-Hobo conditions — shows the cleanest water chemistry signal the river has produced in any of its six sample time points. Cyanobacteria at 4.2% is close to the theoretical background expected in an undisturbed Maine headwater stream. The dominant community — *Monomastix* (19.8%), *Mallomonas* (6.3%), *Chromulina* (4.0%), *Pseudopedinella elastica* (1.8%) — is a cold-water chrysophyte and chlorophyte assemblage typical of clean, soft, slightly acidic Maine drainage. *Nostoc linckia* at 2.4% is the low-flow fingerprint: this cyanobacterium grows in dark crusts on exposed cobble and bedrock, shedding eDNA when water levels drop and expose substrate. The sampler confirmed the Hobo logger was dry and repositioned — the biology and the field observation are in agreement.

The fish community, by contrast, reads warm: Eel 58%, Golden Shiner 30%. Golden Shiner is a warm, still-to-slow-water cyprinid that tolerates a wide temperature range — its dominance at 30% during low flow is not surprising. Eel at 58% during low flow is a concentration effect: as flow drops, eels pool in remaining deep water where eDNA amplifies. Alewife at 0.9% are late-season stragglers; the main run at the Orange River peaked in April–May.

The central ecological finding here: **the algal community says Brook Trout water chemistry conditions exist at this location in June. The fish community says Brook Trout are not here.** This is the most productive way to read that gap. It is not a water quality failure — it is a sampling position problem. The mainstem Orange River at the monitoring point is accessible to warm-water species but drains headwater tributaries where the cold-oligotrophic chemistry found by the algae originates. A sample placed in a small cold tributary upstream of the monitoring point in October or November — when warm-water eDNA shedding is minimal and Brook Trout are most active — is the priority experiment to resolve Brook Trout status in this watershed.

### Native vs. Non-Native

All species native. *Halamphora coffeaeformis* read excluded as likely artifact.
