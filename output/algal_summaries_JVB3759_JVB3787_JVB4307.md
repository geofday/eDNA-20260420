# eDNA Field Reports — Batches JVB3759 · JVB3787 · JVB4307
## 23S rRNA (Algae) + MiFish 12S rRNA (Fish) — Integrated Ecological Narratives

> **This work is funded by trout fishermen, for trout fishermen.**
> Brook Trout (*Salvelinus fontinalis*) is the charismatic canary-in-the-coal-mine
> for cold, clean, well-oxygenated water. Every algal and fish signal below is
> interpreted first through the lens of: *what does this mean for Brook Trout
> and Brook Trout habitat?*
>
> eDNA detects species presence only — it cannot distinguish life history forms,
> ecotypes, or residency from transience. *Salvelinus* sp. in the MiFish output
> is Brook Trout DNA in the water column; wild resident vs. stocked vs. transient
> cannot be resolved without electrofishing, tagging, or otolith data.
> Fish community percentages are re-normalized to fish-only reads.
> H_f = fish Shannon diversity; H_a = algal Shannon diversity (both: −Σ p·ln p).
> Brown Trout (*Salmo trutta*) and Rainbow Trout (*Oncorhynchus mykiss*) are
> introduced/stocked non-native species in New England — their detection is noted
> but not celebrated.

### Ecological Interpretation

The Ellsworth Dam pool in July 2024 is a site of extraordinary anadromous fish activity.
**Alewife at 39.7%** is the dominant signal — this is the spring run in residence.
Alewife (*Alosa pseudoharengus*) ascend the Union River each spring to spawn,
and their July eDNA signal reflects juveniles still in the system and residual DNA
from adults. This is the marine nutrient pump in action: each Alewife that dies
after spawning delivers marine-derived phosphorus, nitrogen, and carbon to a
freshwater system that would otherwise be phosphorus-limited.

**Striped Bass at 10.4%** is a striking finding. Striped Bass are marine apex predators
that ascend coastal rivers to follow baitfish runs. Their eDNA at the dam pool confirms
they are penetrating the Union River system — almost certainly following the Alewife.
A Striped Bass in the dam pool is not an ecological coincidence; it is a predator
tracking its prey. This is the marine-freshwater energy transfer made visible through eDNA.

**No Brook Trout detected.** This is ecologically informative. The July dam pool is
warm, impounded, and dominated by anadromous and warm-water species. Brook Trout require
cold water — typically below 18°C — and the summer stratified conditions of a dam pool
in July are inhospitable to salmonids at that location. If Brook Trout exist in the Union
River watershed, they are upstream in cold tributary headwaters, not in the dam pool in July.

The algal signal confirms the warm impounded character: **Cyanobacteria at 33%**,
led by *Synechococcus* and *Cyanobium* picoplankton. *Halamphora coffeaeformis* at 13%
(a brackish-tolerant diatom) reflects the ionic enrichment of the dam pool and legacy
saline sediment influence. *Mallomonas* (4.2%, soft-water Synurophyte) provides the
counterpoint — the upstream water column retains soft-water Maine character even as the
dam pool concentrates solutes.

**Inferred water chemistry (July 2024):**

| Parameter | Estimate | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Temperature | Warm, stratified | Cyanobacteria + midsummer |
| pH | 6.5–7.5 | Anadromous fish + *Halamphora* |
| Conductivity | Elevated | Dam concentration + *Halamphora* |
| Marine-derived nutrients | Active | Alewife run present |
| Brook Trout habitat | Not at this site in July | No detection; dam pool conditions |

conditions where bloom events become possible. If dissolved oxygen crashes during a bloom,
any cold-water refugia supporting the Alewife staging and any remaining trout habitat
upstream are both at risk.
