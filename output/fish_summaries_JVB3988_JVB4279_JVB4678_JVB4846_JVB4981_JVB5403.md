# eDNA Field Reports — Batches JVB3988 · JVB4279 · JVB4678 · JVB4846 · JVB4981 · JVB5403
## MiFish 12S rRNA (Fish) — Ecological Narratives

**Note on algal data:** These six batches were run as MiFish 12S rRNA (fish) panels only. No 23S rRNA algal panel data is available for any sample in this document. Water chemistry inferences below are based on fish community composition alone and are therefore less precise than the combined fish+algae analyses in the companion v2 document (Batches JVB3759, JVB3787, JVB4307). Algal panel analysis of priority sites in future sampling would substantially strengthen the ecological interpretation.

**This work is funded by trout fishermen, for trout fishermen.** Brook Trout (*Salvelinus fontinalis*) is the charismatic canary-in-the-coal-mine for cold, clean, well-oxygenated water. Every fish signal below is interpreted first through the lens of: *what does this mean for Brook Trout and Brook Trout habitat?*

Standing rules: Fish community percentages are re-normalized to fish-only reads. Non-aquatic species (birds, mammals, reptiles, amphibians) are filtered from all analysis. H(f) = fish Shannon diversity (−Σ p·ln p). eDNA detects species presence — it cannot distinguish life history form, residency, or ecotype. Brown Trout (*Salmo trutta*) and Rainbow Trout (*Oncorhynchus mykiss*) are non-native/stocked species in New England — detected but not celebrated.

# BATCH JVB3988 — East Machias Watershed, ME
## Lab Results: October 4, 2024 · Five Sites

**Sites:** East Machias Rt 9 · Northern Stream · Northern Outlet · Richardson Stream · Beaver Dam Stream

This batch samples a cluster of small coastal Maine streams in the East Machias / Washington County watershed — historically among the last strongholds of wild Atlantic Salmon and Brook Trout in the eastern United States. Washington County streams are part of the federally endangered Gulf of Maine Distinct Population Segment (DPS) of Atlantic Salmon. Sampling was conducted in late summer / early fall, the season when Atlantic Salmon begin their spawning return to natal tributaries.

**Brook Trout status: Absent across all five sites.** The absence is not conclusive — October mainstem and lower-reach sampling during the Alewife fall run may not represent cold headwater tributary conditions where Brook Trout would be expected. Cold-season resamples of the upper reaches of Northern Stream and Richardson Stream are recommended.

No Brook Trout detected — not unexpected for the main stem of Northern Stream in October when the Atlantic Salmon run is moving through. Cold headwater tributaries above this sampling point are the likely Brook Trout habitat; a spring or winter resample in upper reaches would be informative.

Ecological interpretation of the fish community is unreliable: raccoon fecal contamination (36% of raw library) may have suppressed detection of less-abundant fish species via competitive PCR inhibition. The Eel signal (99.3%) is almost certainly real, but the apparent impoverishment to only 2 species should not be taken as the true fish community at this reach.

Rock Bass (*Ambloplites rupestris*, 9.5%) is likewise non-native to New England coastal watersheds — introduced from the Great Lakes drainage. Its presence adds to the invasive species pressure on the native community.

*Cyprinella spiloptera* (Spotfin Shiner, 3.6%) is a Mid-Atlantic/Midwest cyprinid with no confirmed native range in New England. This is most likely a database misassignment of reads from a native cyprinid (Golden Shiner or similar); 7,351 total reads is a moderate-confidence dataset. Flag for QC review; do not treat as an ecological detection.

# Non-Aquatic eDNA Detections — Summary and Methodological Note

## What These Are

MiFish 12S rRNA primers target vertebrate DNA broadly — the barcode captures anything with 12S rDNA, not just fish. Water samples contain trace DNA from every organism that touched the water: birds, mammals, amphibians, and terrestrial plants entering via rain, leaf litter, fecal input, or animal contact. The following non-aquatic detections appeared in raw data across these batches and were **excluded from all fish analysis** by the NON_FISH filter. They are compiled here as a methodological record — not as ecological findings.

## Why Terrestrial eDNA Cannot Be Used as an Ecological Indicator

*Picea* (spruce/fir) DNA appearing in eDNA samples is the clearest illustration of this principle. Spruce-fir forest is present at **100% of the Maine sampling locations** in this study — every site from East Machias to the Narraguagus is surrounded by boreal spruce-fir-hardwood forest. Yet *Picea* eDNA appears in algal reads at **only one site** (Northern Stream) across the JVB3988 batch.

This is not because Northern Stream has more spruce than Beaver Dam Stream or Richardson Stream. It is because **terrestrial eDNA in water samples is stochastic** — a function of random needle-fall, wind direction, runoff events, and local canopy openings at the moment of sampling. The same forest produces different terrestrial eDNA signals at different sample events, and the absence of *Picea* eDNA at a site surrounded by spruce forest tells you nothing about the ecology. It tells you only that the specific needles, pollen, and throughfall that happened to wash into the sample bottle on that specific day did not include enough *Picea* to pass the detection threshold.

**Rule:** Non-aquatic terrestrial eDNA (plants, birds, mammals, amphibians, reptiles) is filtered from analysis, summarized here, and not interpreted ecologically at the site level. The *Struthio* ostrich artifact at School Street is the extreme case — a reference database false positive with no biological meaning whatsoever. The raccoon fecal contamination at Fresh Brook is a genuine non-target capture, but of sample integrity, not ecology. The Painted Turtle at Atwater is real biology — the turtle was there — but is not a fish and not relevant to the fish community analysis.

**Non-aquatics are a collective illustration of eDNA methodology**, not individual ecological findings. They demonstrate that the 12S barcode is a broad-spectrum vertebrate detector, not a fish-specific one, and that environmental filtering and reference database quality are non-trivial analytical challenges in every eDNA study.

## Dietary eDNA Artifacts — Piscivorous Birds

A related but distinct artifact category involves fish eDNA deposited at a site by piscivorous birds (Double-crested Cormorant, Great Blue Heron, Osprey, Kingfisher, Common Tern) via feces. These deposits contain authentic fish DNA — but from fish consumed in a different aquatic environment, not fish present at the sampling site.

The Atwater site (JVB4981 / 2KL87R0I, June 8, 2025) is the clearest example in this dataset. Menhaden (*Brevoortia* spp., 176 reads) and marine prickleback (*Xiphister* spp., 65 reads) — both coastal marine species — appear at a freshwater site miles above the tidal zone. Double-crested Cormorants foraging on menhaden and other coastal fish in Manchester Harbor and adjacent waters transit inland through the MBTS watershed, depositing marine fish DNA wherever they land or roost.

Dietary eDNA artifacts are distinguishable from authentic live-fish signals by geographic impossibility: a coastal schooling marine fish in mid-watershed Sawmill Brook freshwater is not an identification error — it is DNA from a meal consumed elsewhere. This logic also applies to the 85 Alewife reads at the same Atwater event: Alewife are biologically capable of ascending Sawmill Brook, but in June at a de-watered site with confirmed cormorant input, these reads are attributed to dietary artifact rather than live fish.

**Rule:** Detections of species geographically impossible as live fish at the sampling site — especially at low read counts at sites frequented by fish-eating birds — require consideration of cormorant/heron dietary input before ecological interpretation.
